### Analyst Note
American Express reported strong Q1 2025 results, with revenue of $16.97 billion, up 9% year-over-year, driven by 14% billed business growth among millennials and Gen Z customers and 13% international expansion [1]. EPS reached $3.64, exceeding expectations, supported by 18% growth in card fee revenue and the addition of 3.4 million new proprietary cards [1]. These metrics reflect resilient consumer spending amid moderating inflation, with U.S. consumer billed business up 5% and commercial segments showing 8% growth [2]. Credit quality remained solid, with net write-off rates at 2.1%, below historical averages, though provisions for credit losses increased 15% due to higher card loan balances [1].

Looking to Q2 2025, insufficient data exists for detailed results as of this report date, but preliminary indicators from industry trends suggest continued momentum in payment volumes, potentially offset by seasonal travel spending [3]. Management's guidance for full-year 2025 revenue growth of 9-11% and EPS of $13.00-13.50 appears achievable, based on sustained demand for premium products like the Platinum Card [1]. International markets, representing 28% of revenue, are a key driver, with 13% growth in Q1 underscoring expansion in Europe and Asia [1].

The thesis remains intact: American Express benefits from a premium brand and network effects that support above-market growth. We raise our fair value estimate to $280 from $260, incorporating higher revenue projections and margin expansion. This implies a 20x multiple on 2026 EPS, aligned with historical averages [4]. However, medium-term risks include potential economic softening, which could pressure credit metrics; our model assumes 8% revenue CAGR through 2029, with sensitivity to GDP trends. Valuation at 0.83x FVE suggests upside potential, though investors should monitor delinquency trends. Overall, the company's focus on affluent customers positions it well for secular shifts to digital payments, with ROIC averaging 16% over the past three years supporting long-term value creation [1]. ### Business Description
American Express operates as a global payments and financial services company, providing charge and credit card products, travel-related services, and merchant payment processing. Key segments include U.S. Consumer Services (45% of revenue), Commercial Services (25%), International Card Services (20%), and Global Merchant and Network Services (10%) [1]. The company serves individuals, small businesses, and corporations, with a focus on premium offerings. Geographically, the U.S. accounts for approximately 72% of revenue, while international markets, including Europe, Asia-Pacific, and Latin America, contribute 28% [1].

### Business Strategy & Outlook
American Express differentiates through its closed-loop payment network, premium brand positioning, and data-driven customer insights, enabling higher fees and loyalty. The strategy emphasizes acquiring younger demographics, with millennials and Gen Z driving 60% of new consumer accounts, supported by digital enhancements and partnerships like Delta Air Lines [1]. Competitive edges include high switching costs for cardholders and merchants, as well as proprietary rewards programs that foster retention.

Secular trends favor growth, including the shift to cashless payments and rising global travel, projected to increase payment volumes by 7% annually through 2029 [3]. Internationally, the company targets expansion in underpenetrated markets, aiming for 10%+ growth in card fees.

In the medium term (2025-2027), we expect revenue growth of 8-10%, driven by billed business expansion and fee income, though margins may face pressure from marketing investments. Outlook assumes stable economic conditions; a slowdown could reduce spending, but the affluent customer base provides resilience [4].

### Bulls Say / Bears Say

- International billed business up 13%, indicating successful geographic diversification and reduced U.S. dependency [1].

**Bears Say**

- Regulatory scrutiny on interchange fees poses risks to revenue streams [4].  
- Valuation at 18x forward EPS appears elevated relative to peers amid economic uncertainty [2].

### Economic Moat
American Express maintains a wide economic moat, primarily from network effects and intangible assets. The closed-loop model creates a two-sided platform where more cardholders attract merchants, and vice versa, evidenced by 140 million cards in force and acceptance at 80 million locations globally [1]. Brand strength as a premium provider enables fee-based revenue, with card fees comprising 25% of total, up 18% in Q1 2025 [1]. Switching costs are high, as customers invested in rewards ecosystems face inconvenience in changing providers.

Evidence includes sustained ROIC of 16% in 2023-2024, well above cost of capital, and operating margins averaging 20%, superior to peers like Visa (65%) but resilient in credit-focused segments [1][4]. These factors support competitive advantages lasting over 20 years.

### Fair Value and Profit Drivers
Our discounted cash flow model projects a fair value of $280 per share. We forecast revenue CAGR of 8% from 2025-2029, driven by 7% billed business growth and 10% in card fees, based on Q1 2025 trends [1]. Operating margins expand to 22% by 2029 from 21% in 2025, reflecting scale efficiencies and lower credit costs.

WACC is 8.5%, incorporating a 4% risk-free rate, 6% equity risk premium, and beta of 1.2 [4]. Terminal growth is 4%, aligned with global GDP. EPS bridge: 2025 EPS $13.75 starts from 2024's $12.50, adds $1.00 from revenue growth, $0.50 from margins, offset by $0.25 higher taxes. This yields 2026 EPS of $15.00.

Implied multiples: 20x 2025 EPS and 3.5x sales, consistent with 10-year averages [2].

### Risk & Uncertainty
Macroeconomic slowdowns could elevate credit losses, with delinquencies potentially rising 50 basis points in a recession, swinging valuation by 15% [1]. Regulatory risks include caps on late fees or antitrust actions on network practices, as seen in ongoing DOJ cases [4]. ESG factors involve data privacy breaches, given handling of sensitive financial information. Operational risks stem from cybersecurity threats, with potential costs of $500 million per incident based on industry precedents [3]. Uncertainty is rated medium, with fair value range of $210-$350.

### Capital Allocation
Balance sheet is strong, with Tier 1 capital ratio of 10.5% as of Q1 2025, exceeding regulatory requirements [1]. M&A discipline is evident, focusing on bolt-on deals like Resy without overpaying. Dividend payout is 20% of earnings, with yield at 1.2%, and share buybacks totaled $4 billion in 2024, reducing shares by 2% [1]. This prudent approach earns an exemplary rating.
