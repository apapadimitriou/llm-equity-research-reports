### Analyst Note
Johnson & Johnson reported first-quarter 2025 results on April 15, 2025, with revenue of $21.9 billion, exceeding expectations of $21.6 billion, and adjusted EPS of $2.77, surpassing estimates of $2.58 [1]. GAAP EPS was $4.54, up from $1.34 in the prior-year quarter, while free cash flow increased to $3.4 billion from $2.85 billion [1]. The company maintained its full-year 2025 guidance, projecting sales growth of 2-3% and adjusted EPS of $10.50-$10.70 [1]. This follows a solid 2024, where full-year revenue reached $88.8 billion, reflecting 4.3% reported growth and 5.9% operational growth [2].

The thesis for Johnson & Johnson remains centered on its diversified healthcare portfolio, with strength in Innovative Medicine and MedTech segments driving steady growth amid patent expirations for key drugs like Stelara. Q1 performance was bolstered by strong sales in oncology and immunology, offsetting declines in COVID-19 vaccine revenue. Operational growth excluding the vaccine was robust, aligning with secular trends in aging populations and demand for medical devices [2]. However, margins faced pressure from acquired in-process R&D charges, and the company announced a 4.8% dividend increase, marking its 63rd consecutive year of hikes [1].

Looking ahead to Q2 2025, analysts anticipate continued momentum, though macroeconomic headwinds such as inflation in supply chains could temper results. The investment case hinges on pipeline advancements, including recent acquisitions like V-Wave, which contributed to EPS dilution in 2024 [2]. Valuation appears reasonable, with the fair-value estimate of $175 implying modest upside based on projected EPS growth to $10.86 for 2025, an 8.8% year-over-year increase [3]. Risks include regulatory scrutiny on talc litigation and competitive pressures in pharmaceuticals. Overall, the outlook supports a hold recommendation, with emphasis on capital allocation discipline and moat preservation in a maturing industry.

### Business Description
Johnson & Johnson operates as a global healthcare company, developing and manufacturing products in pharmaceuticals, medical devices, and consumer health [4]. Key segments include Innovative Medicine, focusing on oncology, immunology, neuroscience, cardiovascular, and pulmonary hypertension therapies, and MedTech, encompassing surgical tools, orthopedics, vision care, and interventional solutions [4]. The company generates approximately 55% of revenue from the U.S., with significant presence in Europe, Asia-Pacific, and other regions [2].

### Business Strategy & Outlook
Johnson & Johnson leverages its scale and R&D capabilities to maintain leadership in healthcare innovation, investing heavily in pipeline development to address unmet medical needs [4]. The strategy emphasizes acquisitions and partnerships, as evidenced by the 2024 V-Wave deal to bolster cardiovascular offerings [2]. Secular trends such as aging demographics and rising chronic disease prevalence support demand for its products, with operational growth excluding COVID-19 vaccine reaching 7.0% in 2024 [2].

In the medium term, the company faces patent cliffs for drugs like Stelara, but mitigates this through biosimilar defenses and new launches in oncology and immunology [3]. Outlook for 2025-2027 projects steady revenue expansion, driven by MedTech recovery post-pandemic and Innovative Medicine growth at 5-7% annually [3]. However, inflationary pressures and supply chain disruptions could impact margins, with management targeting cost efficiencies to sustain operating margins around 25-27% [2].

### Economic Moat
Johnson & Johnson maintains a wide economic moat, primarily sourced from intangible assets such as patents and brand strength in pharmaceuticals and consumer health, enabling pricing power and market share retention [4]. Evidence includes 2024 operational sales growth of 5.9%, driven by established products despite competitive pressures [2]. Switching costs in MedTech, where surgeons rely on familiar devices, further reinforce the moat, as seen in consistent segment performance [3].
