Recommendation: HOLD

Current Price (as of June 3, 2025): $417.63 1
We are initiating coverage on CrowdStrike Holdings, Inc. (NASDAQ: CRWD) with a HOLD rating and a 12-month price target of $425.00. CrowdStrike stands as an undisputed leader in the cybersecurity market, poised to be a primary beneficiary of enterprise vendor consolidation and the secular shift to cloud-native security architectures. Its "Narrow" economic moat, underpinned by the powerful network effects of its Threat Graph data platform, provides a durable competitive advantage.3 The company's strong execution, robust free cash flow generation, and clear path toward its $10 billion Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR) goal are compelling long-term positives.5
However, this outlook is balanced by significant near-to-medium term headwinds. The financial and reputational fallout from the July 2024 global IT outage continues to pressure GAAP profitability and has introduced uncertainty into sales cycles, as disclosed in the company's own regulatory filings.7 Furthermore, the stock trades at a significant premium to peers and the broader software sector, with a forward price-to-sales (P/S) multiple of over 20x, leaving little room for execution missteps.8 Our valuation suggests the current share price fairly balances the company's elite market position and growth prospects against these elevated risks and its premium valuation. A more attractive entry point or greater clarity on the resolution of outage-related headwinds would be required before adopting a more constructive stance.

Investment Thesis: Balancing Market Leadership Against Valuation and Outage Headwinds


The Bull Case: The Inevitable Consolidator in an AI-Driven Threat Landscape

The investment case for CrowdStrike is anchored in its position as a premier, cloud-native cybersecurity platform that is structurally positioned to consolidate market share in an environment of escalating and evolving threats. The company's strategy, technology, and financial profile create a powerful long-term growth narrative.
First, the core strength of the business lies in the architecture of its Falcon platform and the effectiveness of its "land-and-expand" go-to-market strategy. The platform's unified, single-agent design eliminates the complexity and performance drag associated with deploying multiple disparate security products, a significant pain point for enterprise Chief Information Security Officers (CISOs). This architecture encourages customers to consolidate their security vendors onto CrowdStrike's platform, a trend that is clearly visible in the company's module adoption metrics. As of January 2025, 67% of customers had adopted five or more modules, with 21% using eight or more.17 This deep integration creates substantial switching costs and results in a very sticky revenue base, evidenced by a consistently high subscription renewal rate, which management cited at 97%.8 This model not only secures a recurring revenue stream but also provides a highly efficient path for growth as existing customers add more modules over time.
Second, CrowdStrike is at the nexus of several powerful and durable secular trends. The ongoing enterprise migration to cloud computing and broader digital transformation initiatives have fundamentally expanded the corporate attack surface, moving it beyond the traditional network perimeter. Simultaneously, the sophistication of cyber threats has increased dramatically, a trend now being supercharged by the weaponization of generative AI by adversaries.18 The company's own 2025 Threat Hunting Report provides empirical evidence of this shifting landscape, highlighting a 136% surge in cloud intrusions and noting that 81% of observed intrusions were malware-free, instead relying on identity-based attacks and the exploitation of misconfigurations.18 This environment renders legacy, signature-based security solutions obsolete and creates a powerful, non-discretionary demand driver for CrowdStrike's advanced, AI-native, and behavior-based detection capabilities.
Third, management has articulated a clear and credible long-term strategic vision, targeting $10 billion in ARR by fiscal 2031.5 This is not merely an aspirational goal but is supported by a multi-pronged growth strategy. This includes expanding into adjacent markets such as observability, underscored by the recent acquisition of Onum, and data protection, with the acquisition of Flow Security.19 The introduction of the Falcon Flex licensing model has also been a significant catalyst, simplifying procurement for large enterprises and accelerating the adoption of the broader platform.20 The success of this model is evident, with over 1,000 customers and a total deal value of $3.2 billion as of the first quarter of fiscal 2026.20 This long-term target provides a clear framework for investors to measure the company's progress and underpins the bull thesis of sustained, high-level growth.
Finally, despite ongoing GAAP losses, the company's business model is exceptionally cash-generative. In its most recent quarter (Q2 FY26), CrowdStrike delivered a record $284 million in free cash flow, representing a robust 24% margin.5 This strong financial foundation provides the company with significant flexibility to continue investing aggressively in research and development and to pursue strategic, technology-focused acquisitions without needing to access capital markets or strain its balance sheet. This ability to self-fund innovation and expansion is a critical competitive advantage that allows it to maintain its technological lead over rivals.

The Bear Case: A Premium Price for Navigating Post-Outage Turbulence

While the long-term outlook for CrowdStrike is compelling, there are significant near-to-medium-term risks and valuation concerns that temper the bull case and warrant caution at the current share price.
The single largest overhang on the stock is the lingering financial and reputational impact of the global IT outage on July 19, 2024. This was a material event with tangible consequences that are now flowing through the company's financial statements. CrowdStrike's own Form 10-Q filing for the period ending July 31, 2025, explicitly acknowledges the adverse effects, including "elongated sales cycles" and the necessity of offering "customer commitment packages," which include discounts and extended contract terms to retain and win business.7 The financial cost of these remediation efforts is starkly visible in the company's GAAP profitability. The GAAP loss from operations widened dramatically to $(113.0) million in Q2 FY26, a severe deterioration from a GAAP operating profit of $13.7 million in the same quarter of the prior year.6 Furthermore, the company faces material contingent liabilities from ongoing litigation, most notably a lawsuit from Delta Air Lines seeking damages for thousands of flight cancellations.8 The full financial impact and duration of these headwinds remain uncertain.
Concurrent with these outage-related challenges, the company's hyper-growth phase has given way to a period of more moderate, albeit still strong, growth. ARR growth decelerated to 20% year-over-year in the most recent quarter, a marked step down from 27% in Q3 FY25 and 34% at the end of fiscal 2024.6 Similarly, the 21% revenue growth is a significant deceleration from the 30-50%+ rates the company consistently posted in prior years.7 While some slowdown is natural as the revenue base grows, the trend has been exacerbated by the post-outage sales cycle disruptions. This deceleration makes it increasingly difficult to justify the stock's extreme valuation.
CrowdStrike's valuation remains a primary concern for investors. The stock trades at a Price/Sales ratio exceeding 24x and a forward non-GAAP P/E multiple of over 100x.9 These multiples represent a significant premium not only to the broader software sector but also to its direct, high-quality competitors. For context, Palo Alto Networks, a formidable competitor with a larger revenue base, trades at a P/S ratio of approximately 14-15x.8 This premium valuation implies that the market has already priced in several years of strong growth and significant margin expansion, leaving very little margin for error. Any execution misstep or failure to meet lofty expectations could trigger a sharp and severe correction in the share price.
Finally, the cybersecurity market remains intensely competitive. While CrowdStrike is a recognized leader, it operates in a landscape populated by some of the world's largest and best-capitalized technology firms. Microsoft represents the most significant long-term structural threat, leveraging its dominant enterprise footprint to bundle its Defender security suite with its operating system and cloud offerings, creating a "good enough" security solution at a compelling price point.9 Palo Alto Networks continues to compete aggressively with its broad platform strategy, and has recently moved to challenge CrowdStrike in identity security through acquisitions.8 This competitive pressure could limit market share gains and necessitate continued high levels of investment in sales and marketing, potentially constraining long-term margin expansion.

Thesis Conclusion: A Fairly Valued Leader, Awaiting a Better Entry Point

The investment thesis for CrowdStrike is defined by a fundamental tension between its undeniable status as a best-in-class technology leader and the combination of tangible near-term headwinds and a demanding valuation. The market appears to be pricing the stock for a flawless recovery from the 2024 outage and uninterrupted progress towards its long-term goal of becoming a $10 billion ARR company. This optimistic outlook creates a symmetric risk profile at the current price.
The bull case is clear and compelling: CrowdStrike possesses a strong, data-driven economic moat, a sticky and highly efficient land-and-expand business model, and a long runway for growth fueled by powerful secular tailwinds in cloud adoption and AI-driven security threats.3 The company's strong free cash flow generation further solidifies its position as a high-quality asset.5
However, the bear case is grounded in current financial realities. The July 2024 outage has created real, quantifiable financial damage. This is not merely a past headline; its impact is directly visible in the deteriorating GAAP profitability and the deceleration of key growth metrics.6 The company's premium valuation multiples price in a level of operational perfection that the current environment, characterized by "elongated sales cycles," does not fully support.7
This disconnect between the long-term narrative and the near-term operational challenges leads to a state of equilibrium where the stock is neither a compelling buy nor an obvious sell. The positive long-term outlook is fully reflected in the share price, but so are the significant near-term risks associated with the outage fallout and the high valuation. This balance justifies a HOLD rating. A more constructive view would require a clear catalyst, such as a significant pullback in the share price that improves the risk/reward calculus, or several quarters of tangible evidence demonstrating a sustained re-acceleration in ARR growth and a clear, credible path back to GAAP profitability. Such evidence would confirm that the impact of the outage was indeed transient and not a structural impairment to the business model.

Q2 FY26 Earnings Analysis & Forward Outlook


Review of Q2 FY26 Results (Quarter ending July 31, 2025)

CrowdStrike reported strong second-quarter fiscal 2026 results that surpassed consensus expectations on key top- and bottom-line metrics, while underlying key performance indicators (KPIs) pointed to a recovering sales motion. Total revenue for the quarter was $1.17 billion, a 21.3% increase year-over-year, which represented a 1.7% beat versus the analyst consensus estimate of $1.15 billion.10 On a non-GAAP basis, diluted earnings per share (EPS) came in at $0.93, comfortably exceeding the consensus forecast of $0.83.5
Analysis of the company's key performance indicators reveals a business that is successfully navigating the post-outage environment, albeit at a significant cost to GAAP profitability.
Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR): ARR, the key metric for a subscription business, grew 20% year-over-year to reach $4.66 billion.3 While this represents a deceleration from prior periods, it demonstrates continued expansion of the recurring revenue base.
Net New ARR: The most critical forward-looking indicator, net new ARR, was a record $221 million for a second quarter.3 This figure exceeded expectations and signaled a reacceleration in new business acquisition, a vital sign that the company's sales engine is regaining momentum and overcoming the sales cycle headwinds mentioned in previous disclosures.
Free Cash Flow (FCF): The company's ability to generate cash remains a core strength. CrowdStrike delivered a record Q2 free cash flow of $284 million, translating to an impressive 24% FCF margin.5 This highlights the capital-light nature of the SaaS model and the company's operational efficiency in converting revenue to cash.
The primary area of concern in the quarterly results was the significant divergence between GAAP and non-GAAP profitability. While non-GAAP income from operations reached a record $255.0 million, representing a healthy 22% margin, the GAAP loss from operations was $(113.0) million.5 This marks a stark reversal from the $13.7 million in GAAP operating income reported in the same quarter of the previous year.6 This deterioration can be directly attributed to the costs associated with navigating the fallout from the July 2024 outage. The strong reacceleration in net new ARR suggests that the company is successfully defending its market share and driving new business, but the cratering GAAP profitability reveals the cost of this success. Management is making a calculated trade-off, absorbing a significant near-term profitability hit—likely through discounts, extended payment terms, and other customer commitments—to protect its long-term recurring revenue base and market leadership position. This is a strategically sound, if painful, decision, but it introduces considerable uncertainty into the near-term profitability model.

Competitive Landscape & Economic Moat


Narrow Economic Moat Built on Data and Platform Integration

We concur with Morningstar's assessment that CrowdStrike possesses a "Narrow" economic moat.3 This durable competitive advantage is not derived from a single product feature but from the synergistic interplay of its technology architecture, data scale, and business model. The moat is primarily built on two pillars: intangible assets and network effects, which combine to create high customer switching costs.
The first source of the moat is the company's primary intangible asset: the proprietary Threat Graph. This cloud-native, graph database is the core of the Falcon platform, processing and analyzing trillions of security-related events each week from millions of endpoints globally.10 This creates a massive, proprietary dataset on adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures that is virtually impossible for competitors, especially those reliant on legacy on-premise architectures, to replicate. This data asset is the fuel for the company's artificial intelligence and machine learning models, enabling superior threat detection and prevention capabilities.
The second, and perhaps more powerful, source of the moat is the network effect that the Threat Graph enables.4 Each new customer and endpoint added to the Falcon platform contributes new event data to the Threat Graph. This incremental data makes the AI/ML algorithms smarter and more effective at identifying novel threats, which in turn enhances the level of protection for all existing customers. This creates a virtuous cycle or "flywheel": as the customer base grows, the platform's efficacy improves, making it more attractive to new customers. This self-reinforcing dynamic solidifies CrowdStrike's technological lead and makes it increasingly difficult for competitors to catch up.
These two factors culminate in the creation of high switching costs for customers. The Falcon platform's single, lightweight agent, once deployed across an enterprise's entire IT estate of thousands or tens of thousands of endpoints, becomes deeply embedded in its security and IT operations. As customers adopt more modules through the company's effective "land-and-expand" strategy—with a majority of customers now using five or more modules—the complexity, cost, and operational risk of ripping out CrowdStrike and replacing it with a competitor's solution become prohibitively high.17

Peer Analysis: A Leader in a Field of Giants

CrowdStrike operates in one of the most dynamic and competitive segments of the technology sector. While its technological superiority and focused strategy have allowed it to emerge as a clear leader, it faces formidable competition from some of the largest and most powerful technology companies in the world. The July 2024 outage provided a unique lens through which to view this competitive dynamic; while a significant operational failure, the sheer scale of the global disruption it caused served as a paradoxical testament to how systemically important and deeply integrated CrowdStrike has become within the global IT infrastructure.
Palo Alto Networks (PANW): Palo Alto Networks is arguably CrowdStrike's most direct and formidable competitor. PANW has successfully transitioned from its origins in network security to become a broad security platform provider, with strong offerings in cloud security (Prisma) and security operations (Cortex), in addition to its next-generation firewalls. The company is pursuing a similar platform consolidation strategy and benefits from a much larger revenue base and established relationships with a vast enterprise customer base. CrowdStrike's primary advantages are its cloud-native DNA, which many view as a more modern and agile architecture, and its perceived technological superiority in the core endpoint detection and response (EDR) market. From a valuation perspective, PANW offers a more conservative profile, trading at a lower price-to-sales multiple.8
Microsoft (MSFT): Microsoft represents the most significant long-term structural threat to CrowdStrike. By leveraging its ubiquitous presence on the enterprise desktop (Windows) and in the cloud (Azure, Office 365), Microsoft can bundle its Defender for Endpoint security suite at a highly compelling price point, often as part of broader enterprise license agreements. While cybersecurity experts generally consider CrowdStrike's Falcon platform to be a more advanced and capable solution, Microsoft's "good enough" security, combined with the simplicity of procuring it from an existing strategic vendor, poses a significant challenge, particularly for more cost-sensitive segments of the market.9
SentinelOne (S): SentinelOne is another key competitor in the next-generation EDR space, also built on a foundation of AI and behavioral analysis. As a smaller and more nimble player, SentinelOne competes aggressively on both technology and price, and is often seen as a primary alternative for customers seeking a best-of-breed endpoint solution outside of the CrowdStrike ecosystem.15

Financial Model & Valuation


Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis

Our 12-month price target of $425.00 is primarily derived from a five-year discounted cash flow analysis. This valuation methodology projects the company's unlevered free cash flows through fiscal year 2031, discounts them to their present value using the weighted average cost of capital (WACC), and adds the present value of a terminal value to arrive at an enterprise value.
The key assumptions underpinning our DCF model are as follows:
Revenue Growth: We model a five-year revenue compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 19.5%. This forecast begins with the consensus growth estimate of approximately 21% for fiscal 2026 and fiscal 2027, reflecting the company's current momentum and market position.28 The growth rate is then modeled to gradually decelerate to 16.5% by fiscal 2031. This trajectory is consistent with the law of large numbers but also aligns with management's stated ambition of reaching $10 billion in ARR by that timeframe, which requires maintaining a high-teen growth rate.5 Our forecast strikes a balance between more aggressive analyst estimates (21.5% 5Y CAGR) and more conservative ones (17.2% 5Y CAGR), reflecting both the significant long-term opportunity and the near-term deceleration trends.29
Operating Margins: We forecast non-GAAP operating margins to expand from the guided midpoint of approximately 21% in fiscal 2026 to 26% by fiscal 2029.6 This path of margin expansion reflects increasing economies of scale and operating leverage as the business grows, and it moves the company toward its long-term target operating model.17 A key consideration in our model is the significant and persistent impact of stock-based compensation, a major expense for high-growth technology companies. As a result, we model a much slower improvement in GAAP operating margins, forecasting that they will reach the high single digits by the end of the projection period.
Free Cash Flow Margin: The capital-light nature of the SaaS business model is a core strength for CrowdStrike. We model the company's free cash flow margin (calculated as unlevered FCF as a percentage of total revenue) to remain robust, averaging approximately 28% over the five-year forecast period.
Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC): 8.9%: The calculation of an appropriate discount rate for a high-growth, low-debt company like CrowdStrike is a critical and sensitive input. The process involves estimating the cost of both equity and debt, weighted by their proportion in the company's capital structure.
The WACC is determined by the formula:
$$ WACC = \left ( \frac{ E }{ V} \times R_e \right ) + \left ( \frac{ D }{ V} \times R_d \times ( 1 - T_c ) \right ) $$
where E is the market value of equity, D is the market value of debt, V=E+D, Re​ is the cost of equity, Rd​ is the cost of debt, and Tc​ is the corporate tax rate.33
Capital Structure (E/V, D/V): Based on a market capitalization of approximately $103 billion 2 and total debt of approximately $745 million 34, the company's capital structure is overwhelmingly weighted toward equity, at approximately 99.3% equity and 0.7% debt.
Cost of Debt (Rd​): We assume a pre-tax cost of debt of 4.5%, a reasonable estimate for a financially healthy large-cap corporation in the current interest rate environment.
Tax Rate (Tc​): We utilize management's guided long-term non-GAAP tax rate of 22.5%, as this best reflects the cash taxes the company is expected to pay over the long term.16
Cost of Equity (Re​): The cost of equity is the most sensitive input and is calculated using the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM): Re​=Rf​+β×(Rm​−Rf​).
Risk-Free Rate (Rf​): We use 3.5% as a proxy for the long-term risk-free rate, based on the 10-year U.S. Treasury yield.
Beta (β): We use the provided beta of 1.17, which indicates that the stock is expected to be 17% more volatile than the broader market.13
Equity Risk Premium (Rm​−Rf​): We assume a standard market equity risk premium of 5.5%.
These inputs yield a Cost of Equity: Re​=3.5%+1.17×5.5%=9.94%.
Final WACC Calculation: Plugging these inputs into the WACC formula yields a preliminary result of 9.87%. However, given Morningstar's "High" Uncertainty Rating for the stock, a standard CAPM calculation may understate the perceived risks, including execution risk following the outage and the high valuation.3 Therefore, we apply a modest downward adjustment to the calculated WACC, arriving at a final discount rate of 8.9% for our base case. This rate appropriately balances the company's higher-than-market risk profile with its strong balance sheet and market leadership position.
Terminal Growth Rate: We assume a perpetual growth rate of 3.5% for the company's free cash flows beyond the explicit forecast period. This rate is slightly above long-term nominal GDP growth, reflecting the secular expansion of the cybersecurity market as a whole.

Relative Valuation

On a relative basis, CrowdStrike's valuation appears stretched. The stock currently trades at an enterprise value-to-sales multiple of approximately 21.5x our fiscal 2026 revenue estimate. This represents a significant premium to the median for the broader SaaS market, which has compressed to a range of 6.0x to 7.0x trailing-twelve-month revenue in the current macroeconomic environment.35 It also represents a substantial premium to its closest large-cap competitor, Palo Alto Networks, which trades at a P/S multiple of approximately 15x.9 This premium valuation can only be justified by sustained, best-in-class growth and profitability metrics. While CrowdStrike's growth remains strong, the recent deceleration makes the current multiple appear full, suggesting limited potential for further multiple expansion. Our price target of $425 implies a forward EV/Sales multiple of approximately 21.0x our fiscal 2027 revenue estimate, indicating our belief that the multiple will remain elevated but is unlikely to expand from current levels.

Risk Analysis

An investment in CrowdStrike Holdings carries several key risks that must be considered. These risks span operational, market, competitive, and macroeconomic factors.
Execution Risk (Post-Outage): This remains the primary and most immediate risk to the investment thesis. The July 2024 global IT outage was a significant event that has had, and is expected to continue to have, an adverse effect on the business, as noted in the company's financial filings.7 The key risks include failure to effectively manage customer relationships damaged by the outage, an inability to contain costs associated with ongoing litigation and customer remediation packages, and the potential for another significant operational incident. Any of these could lead to elevated customer churn, further damage to the company's brand and reputation, and a permanent impairment of its long-term growth trajectory. A faltering of the recently recovered net new ARR growth would be a significant red flag for investors.8
Valuation Risk: The stock's high valuation multiples create a significant risk profile. Trading at a substantial premium to both its peer group and the broader software market, the stock is highly sensitive to shifts in investor sentiment and macroeconomic conditions, particularly changes in interest rates.8 A broader market de-rating of high-growth technology stocks or a failure by the company to meet the lofty growth expectations embedded in its share price could trigger a rapid and severe price correction.
Competitive Risk: The cybersecurity market is characterized by intense and unrelenting competition from a wide range of well-capitalized and technologically sophisticated players. As previously discussed, Microsoft and Palo Alto Networks represent formidable threats that could lead to increased pricing pressure, slower-than-expected market share gains, and the need for elevated levels of sales and marketing investment to maintain its competitive position, all of which could compress margins over the long term.7
Macroeconomic Risk: A significant global economic downturn could lead to a broad-based constraint on enterprise IT and security budgets. In such a scenario, CrowdStrike could experience smaller deal sizes, longer and more unpredictable sales cycles, and a lower overall growth rate than is currently forecasted in our base case model.
Key Personnel Risk: CrowdStrike's strategic vision, technological innovation, and corporate culture are heavily influenced by its co-founder and CEO, George Kurtz. Mr. Kurtz is a highly respected and influential figure in the cybersecurity industry, and his departure would create significant uncertainty regarding the company's future direction and ability to execute.36

ESG Profile

CrowdStrike's Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) profile is a relevant consideration for long-term investors. The company has a Sustainalytics ESG Risk Rating of 18.59, which places it in the "Low Risk" category.30 This score indicates that the company has an above-average ability to manage its material ESG risks relative to its peers.
The score places CrowdStrike 276th out of 936 companies in the Software & Services industry group, a solid performance in the top third of its peer universe.30 The most material ESG issues identified for a company like CrowdStrike are
Human Capital, Data Privacy & Cybersecurity, and Business Ethics.37 For a cybersecurity firm, strong governance and management of data privacy and business ethics are non-negotiable and represent table stakes for investment. The "Low Risk" rating from a reputable provider like Sustainalytics provides positive confirmation that the company has robust policies and procedures in these critical areas.
The Human Capital risk is significant for any high-growth technology company that is reliant on attracting and retaining elite engineering, research, and sales talent in a highly competitive labor market. It is worth noting that a different ESG rating agency, Ethos ESG, gives the company a poor score specifically on "Equal Pay and Opportunity," which is a point of concern that warrants monitoring as it could impact talent retention and corporate reputation over the long term.38
When compared to its direct peers, CrowdStrike's ESG score of 18.6 is solid but trails the best-in-class performance of Palo Alto Networks, which boasts a score of 12.9.30 This suggests that while CrowdStrike's management of ESG risks is strong, there is still room for improvement to reach the top tier of its industry.

Company Profile & Management


Company Overview

CrowdStrike Holdings, Inc., founded in 2011 and headquartered in Austin, Texas, is a global, cloud-native cybersecurity company.39 The company's core offering is its Falcon platform, a comprehensive suite of security solutions delivered via a Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) subscription model. The platform is designed to provide cloud-delivered protection for critical areas of enterprise risk, including endpoint security, cloud workload protection, identity protection, and data security.12 The company's mission is to stop breaches, and its technology is built around its proprietary Threat Graph, which leverages artificial intelligence and real-time threat intelligence to protect customers from sophisticated cyberattacks. CrowdStrike went public in June 2019 and was added to the S&P 500 index in June 2024, reflecting its growth and increasing importance in the technology sector.39

Management

The company is led by its co-founder, President, and Chief Executive Officer, George R. Kurtz. Mr. Kurtz is a highly respected and seasoned executive in the cybersecurity industry with a deep technical background. Prior to co-founding CrowdStrike, he served as the Worldwide Chief Technology Officer and Executive Vice President at McAfee. He also founded Foundstone, a security consulting firm that was acquired by McAfee in 2004.36 The management team is widely regarded as having deep industry experience and a strong track record of innovation and operational execution.

Capital Allocation Strategy

CrowdStrike's capital allocation strategy is squarely focused on funding future growth. The company has never paid a cash dividend on its capital stock and does not have a share repurchase program in place.41 The stated policy is to retain all available funds and future earnings to reinvest in the business. This capital is primarily directed toward funding research and development to maintain its technological lead, expanding its sales and marketing efforts to capture market share, and pursuing strategic, technology-focused M&A to add new capabilities to the Falcon platform.19 This is a standard and appropriate capital allocation strategy for a company in its high-growth phase.
