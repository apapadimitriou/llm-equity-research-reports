## Pharmaceutical Segment Dynamics and Patent Cliff Impact

The pharmaceutical segment, operating under the Innovative Medicine business, represents the largest component of Johnson & Johnson's revenue base and faces the most significant near-term challenges from patent cliff dynamics. The segment's performance in Q1 2025 showed operational sales growth of 4.2% to $13.9 billion, demonstrating resilience despite early signs of Stelara biosimilar pressure in international markets[13]. However, the entry of Stelara biosimilars in the U.S. market beginning in January 2025 represents a fundamental shift in the competitive landscape that will significantly impact Q2 2025 and subsequent quarters.

Stelara, Johnson & Johnson's immunology blockbuster that generated $9.7 billion in global sales in 2024, faces the full impact of U.S. biosimilar competition for the first time in Q2 2025[9]. The company had successfully delayed biosimilar entry through patent settlements with multiple manufacturers, but these protection mechanisms expired at the end of 2024. Industry analysts expect the biosimilar impact to follow a pattern similar to AbbVie's experience with Humira, where market share erosion occurred gradually rather than precipitously due to payer contracting dynamics, rebate strategies, and patient switching inertia[10]. However, even a gradual erosion pattern suggests Stelara could experience revenue declines of $3 billion or more in 2025, with Q2 2025 marking the beginning of meaningful impact[5].

The company's strategy to mitigate Stelara's patent cliff involves aggressive market protection tactics combined with portfolio diversification through newer immunology assets. Management has indicated willingness to implement pricing strategies and rebate programs to maintain market share, similar to AbbVie's approach with Humira[10]. This strategy may preserve revenue in the near term but could pressure margins as the company invests more heavily in market access and patient assistance programs. Additionally, Johnson & Johnson is positioning Tremfya, its anti-IL-23 biologic, for expansion into inflammatory bowel disease indications, having received approval for ulcerative colitis and expecting approval for Crohn's disease[10].

The pipeline strength in immunology provides some offset to Stelara's decline, with multiple assets in development including the oral IL-23 inhibitor JNJ-2113, which management views as a potential market disruptor given its oral administration route compared to current injectable biologics[10]. The company expects to have 10 or more assets with peak sales potential exceeding $5 billion by 2030, including several immunology candidates that could help offset Stelara's revenue decline over time[19].
